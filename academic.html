Research Statement

My research program investigates a fundamental question in legal doctrine: when courts apply complex, multi-element legal tests, do all the prescribed factors actually matter? Using large-scale empirical analysis powered by computational methods, I study what I call the "multifactor collapse hypothesis," the theory that elaborate balancing tests routinely reduce to a small number of operative factors in practice, even as courts insist on holistic consideration of all elements. If this hypothesis holds across doctrinal areas, it carries significant implications for judicial economy, practitioner strategy, legal education, and legislative design.

My inaugural study tested this hypothesis in trademark law, analyzing approximately 4,000 Trademark Trial and Appeal Board decisions applying the thirteen-factor DuPont likelihood-of-confusion test. The results were striking: only two of thirteen factors predict outcomes with 99.55% accuracy. This finding, published in the Fordham Intellectual Property, Media & Entertainment Law Journal, challenges fifty years of established doctrine and has attracted attention from practitioners, trade press, and the Supreme Court bar. I am now extending this analysis to federal circuit court confusion decisions to determine whether the same collapse pattern holds across the appellate judiciary.

More broadly, my methodology, which leverages large language models to code legal decisions at a scale previously impractical for individual researchers, is designed to be portable across doctrinal areas. Multifactor tests pervade American law, from the fair use analysis in copyright to the reasonableness inquiry in Fourth Amendment search-and-seizure to the balancing frameworks in dormant Commerce Clause and due process jurisprudence. My long-term research agenda aims to map the gap between doctrine-as-written and doctrine-as-applied across these domains, contributing to a richer empirical understanding of how legal rules actually function in practice.

Works in Progress

Multifactor Mythology: The Fiction of Holistic Adjudication
Argues that multifactor balancing tests across nine doctrinal domains have collapsed to one or two operative factors, and evaluates the gap between stated and applied doctrine as a legal fiction that must now justify its cost.
Confusion by Default: Mass Enforcement and the Multifactor Ritual
Documents the rise of default-judgment trademark enforcement and shows that multifactor confusion analysis in defaulted cases operates as ritual rather than adjudication, proposing a candid standard for uncontested records.
One Standard, Two Tribunals: Testing B&B Hardware's Equivalence Premise
Tests B&B Hardware's premise that TTAB and federal-court confusion analyses are materially the same, drawing on more than 13,000 coded factor applications to show that the standard transports across forums but the evidence does not.
After Confusion: What Winning a Trademark Case Gets You
Follows trademark litigation past liability into remedies, showing that headline post-eBay remedy trends are largely artifacts of docket composition and that a growing share of prevailing plaintiffs obtain no remedy at all.

Teaching

Southern Illinois University, Simmons School of Law

I teach core doctrinal courses in Contracts and Property and advanced courses in Business Organizations and Intellectual Property, covering contract formation, performance, and remedies; estates in land and modern property doctrine; and agency law, partnerships, corporations, and LLCs. My teaching emphasizes practical application alongside doctrinal rigor, preparing students for both the bar exam and professional practice, and my Intellectual Property teaching draws on extensive practice experience to connect classroom concepts to real-world IP strategy. I also serve as faculty advisor to the Intellectual Property Law Society.
